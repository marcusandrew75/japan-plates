02 · The Vermillion Path

千本鳥居
Ten Thousand Gates

Senbon Torii — Fushimi Inari-taisha, Kyoto

Beneath the cedar slopes of Mount Inari, thousands of vermillion gates lean into one another until they become a tunnel of light. Each torii marks the threshold between the ordinary world and the sacred — pass through one, and you have already left something behind.

The gates are donated: every pillar carries the black-inked name of the merchant or family who offered it, a prayer for prosperity written in lacquer and cedar. The practice dates to the Edo period, and the mountain has been accepting gates faster than weather can retire them ever since. Walk the full circuit and the light strobes red-shadow-red for four kilometres.

Inari is the kami of rice, sake, and commerce, attended by stone foxes — kitsune — who carry keys to the granary in their mouths. The vermillion itself is no accident: the pigment, once mixed with mercury, was believed to repel decay and evil alike. In this print, the artist tilts the camera skyward so the gates crowd the frame like masts, and hangs a snow-flecked red sun behind the cedars.

The gate does not ask where you are going.
It only marks that you have chosen to go. — Pilgrim proverb, Mount Inari
03 · The Green Cathedral

竹林
The Bamboo Grove

Chikurin — Arashiyama, western Kyoto

Step off the road at Arashiyama and the temperature drops. Mōsō bamboo climbs twenty metres before it leafs, so the grove becomes a hall of green columns with the canopy for a ceiling — daylight arrives already filtered, the colour of shallow water.

Keep scrolling — the scroll unrolls sideways from here, the way an emakimono handscroll is read.

Plate III · The Thicket

Bamboo is a grass, not a tree — and it behaves like one. A mōsō shoot can rise a full metre in a day; you can sit beside it and watch it grow. Yet the same stalk bends to the ground under snow and stands back up, which is why bamboo, with pine and plum, is one of the Three Friends of Winter.

Sound is the grove's other art. The Ministry of the Environment lists the knock and creak of Arashiyama's swaying culms among the 100 Soundscapes of Japan.

秋Aki — Autumn

花見 · Hanami

The sakura front is tracked on the evening news like weather, moving north at walking pace from Kyūshū to Hokkaidō. Full bloom lasts scarcely a week — which is the point. The blossoms teach mono no aware, the gentle ache of things that pass. Note the bird pausing on the bough: even it seems to know to sit still.

紅葉狩り · Momijigari

Autumn runs the same journey in reverse — the crimson front descends from the north as maples turn. Momijigari, "hunting red leaves," has been a pastime since the Heian court. Where spring is a crowd under falling petals, autumn is quieter: one twisted maple, an amber valley, and leaves letting go one at a time.

05 · Beneath the Surface

The carp that climbed the waterfall

Chinese legend says a carp that fights its way up the falls at Dragon Gate becomes a dragon — which is why the koi stands for perseverance, and why paper carp streamers fly for Children's Day each May. The fish earns its myth: koi routinely live half a century, and one scarlet koi named Hanako was ring-dated to 226 years.

The ornamental breeds — nishikigoi, "brocaded carp" — began as a rice farmer's sideline in the snow valleys of Niigata around 1820. A mutation of colour in a food carp, kept instead of eaten, became a living jewel trade where single fish now change hands for the price of a house.

In both prints the artist follows ukiyo-e convention: the pond is seen flat from above, the water told entirely through engraved line — concentric rings in the dark pond, combed swirls in the pale one — so the fish seem to float on pattern rather than depth.

06 · Festival Night

提灯の夜
Night of a Thousand Lanterns

Chōchin — paper, bamboo, and a single flame

A chōchin is almost nothing: a spiral of split bamboo, a skin of washi paper, a flame. Collapsed, it folds flat as a fan; lit, it becomes architecture. The kanji brushed on its side name a shrine, a shop, a family — so a street of lanterns reads like a directory of the neighbourhood, written in light.

Summer is their season. During Obon, lanterns are lit to guide ancestors home and, on the last night, set adrift on rivers to see them off again — tōrō nagashi, a current of small fires. And outside any izakaya, one red lantern — the akachōchin — still means what it has meant for three hundred years: the kitchen is open, come in out of the dark.

07 · Interlude — Plate X · The Mountain Temple

五重塔 · The Five-Storey Pagoda

Each roof is one of the five elements — earth, water, fire, wind, void — stacked toward the sky, with the ringed spire counting nine heavens above them. The oldest, at Hōryū-ji, has stood since around 700: among the oldest wooden buildings on Earth.

Their secret is looseness. The storeys are not fixed to each other but hang around a free-floating central pillar — the shinbashira — so in an earthquake each floor sways against its neighbours and the tower shrugs the shock away. Fourteen centuries of tremors; almost no pagoda has ever fallen to one.

08 · The Patient Arts

道 — The Way of Small Things

Some arts are finished in a stroke. These two are never finished at all — a bowl of tea whisked in four minutes, a tree shaped over four hundred years.
Plate XI · The Bowl
茶道 · Sadō — The Way of Tea

一期一会One meeting, one chance

The tea ceremony is four hundred years of choreography in service of one idea: ichi-go ichi-e — this gathering, these guests, this bowl, will never occur again, so attend to it completely. Sen no Rikyū pared the practice to a two-mat hut and asked, in effect, how little a moment needs in order to be enough.

Every object in the print is prescribed: the chawan turned twice before drinking, the chasen whisk carved from a single node of bamboo into a hundred springy tines, the matcha itself — shade-grown leaf, stone-milled to powder — whisked not stirred, until the foam draws its own spiral like a raked garden.

Plate XII · The Tree
盆栽 · Bonsai — A Tree in a Tray

木を育てるGrowing time itself

A bonsai is not a dwarf species — it is an ordinary tree persuaded, wire by wire and season by season, to live at the scale of a windowsill. The oldest specimens in Japan have been in continuous training for over a thousand years, passed between generations of keepers who will each see only a fraction of the work.

The ideal is wabi-sabi: asymmetry, age, imperfection worn openly. A masterpiece leans as this one does — the S-curve trunk called moyogi — and must look not made but weathered, as if the tray had simply held a mountainside for centuries.

09 · The Last Plate

武士道

Bushidō — the way of the warrior. The mask scowls so the man beneath it never has to.

The armour in the final plate is ō-yoroi — "great armour" — hundreds of lacquered iron scales laced with silk cord, light enough to fight in from horseback. The horned kabuto and snarling menpō mask were psychology as much as protection: a daimyō's silhouette had to be legible, and terrifying, across a battlefield.

By the long peace of the Edo period the samurai had become administrators and poets who wore two swords to the office — and the code, bushidō, was written down mostly after the fighting ended: rectitude, courage, benevolence, respect, honesty, honour, loyalty. The warrior became an idea, and ideas are harder to retire.
